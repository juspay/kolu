Dynamic Workflows for determinism and codex for a second model family? This
note is the answer to that question, researched for

*Method: a Claude Code dynamic workflow ran 4 parallel evidence gatherers
(kolu's debate internals, agency's documented failure history, Workflow-tool
capabilities, the academic/practitioner literature), then 3 independent
designers from different angles, 9 adversarial critics (cost, quality,
portability — three per design), and 2 judges with opposing priorities

Both judges independently ranked the same design first.*

## TL;DR

<Callout kind="accent" title="Verdict">
**Don't debate to consensus; verify with a cold kill-round.** The measured
payoff of multi-model review comes from **model diversity, not from the
debating** — and rounds past the first measurably *degrade* outcomes
(sycophantic convergence, bias amplification, unanimous-wrong consensus).
The best-evidenced shape for review is: **independent parallel generation →
merge with stable finding ids → ONE cold cross-model verification round on
claims-only packets → an evidence gate that computes dispositions → serial
apply → one bounded re-review.** Agreement between models is *not* evidence;
only code citations and execution are. For kolu specifically: `/be-review`
should run **lens before codex** (so the cross-model debate reviews the
structural rewrites), thread `--rationale` into **adjudication, not
generation**, and both debate skills should **cap rounds and gate concessions
on cited evidence** — <Issue n={1222} /> is the in-house preview of what the
literature predicts.
</Callout>

## What the evidence says

Four research threads, fact-checked against primary sources where the claims
are load-bearing. Secondhand numbers (search-summary provenance) are flagged;
directions are corroborated across multiple sources even where exact figures
are not.

### Diversity, not debate, is where the measured payoff lives

The foundational multi-agent-debate result
([Du et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14325)) shows 3 agents × 2
rounds with majority vote beating single-model chain-of-thought on math,
factuality, and MMLU. But the controlled replication literature is sobering:

- [Smit et al., "Should we be going MAD?"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.17371)
  found multi-agent debate **does not reliably outperform self-consistency or
  ensembling at comparable cost** without careful tuning. The most promising
  knob was *agreement modulation* — controlling how easily agents concede.
- A [controlled study of MAD in logical reasoning](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.07784)
  found debate adds value **only when agents initially disagree**; when
  initial answers are similar, extra rounds add cost, not signal.
- A follow-up to Du et al. ([Diversity of Thought](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.12853v1))
  reports that simply **aggregating three models' independent answers without
  debate (69%) beat letting them debate (66%)** — secondhand numbers,
  directionally consistent with everything else.
- [ReConcile](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.13007) — a round-table of
  *heterogeneous* models with confidence-weighted voting, up to +11.4% over
  baselines — **explicitly attributes its gains to model diversity, not the
  discussion protocol**. This is the strongest academic support for the
  Claude+codex premise: the payoff comes from the second model family, and
  most of it is collectable *without any debate at all*.

### More rounds make consensus worse, not better

This is the part that cuts directly against "no round cap, no deadlock exit":

- A 2026 study of multi-agent deliberation ([arXiv 2604.07667](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07667))
  reports that by round 3, **~24% of initially-disputed questions end in
  unanimous *wrong* consensus**, increasing monotonically with rounds.
  ["Not All Flips Are Conformity"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00820)
  decomposes stance changes: strict conformity (~29% of flips) is
  **predominantly harmful (57–77% correct→wrong)**, and agents adopt errors
  even from vacuous reasoning 20–39% of the time. (Both secondhand; the
  direction is corroborated.)
- [Judging with Many Minds (EMNLP 2025 Findings)](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.941/)
  found multi-agent **debate amplifies judge biases sharply after the first
  round** (position, verbosity, bandwagon) and the amplification persists;
  **meta-judge setups resist bias better than debate**. Survey literature
  agrees gains saturate at round 2, marginal at round 3.
- LLM-as-judge baselines are themselves shaky: position bias flips pairwise
  code judgments on order swap more than 10% of the time
  ([Judging the Judges](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07791)); self-preference
  bias correlates with self-recognition
  ([NeurIPS 2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.21819v2)) — a direct argument
  for **cross-family** review over same-family second passes.
- Structural judgments have no oracle — and the domains *without* checkable
  answers are precisely where deliberation converged wrong most often. The
  lens debates operate exactly there.

Worth stating carefully: `/lens-debate`'s "why deadlock is not possible"
argument (<Cite file=".apm/skills/lens-debate/SKILL.md" />) is about
*termination* — disinterested lenses with no authorship stake do converge.
The literature's complaint is about *correctness*: they converge, and a
measured fraction of the time they converge **wrong**, at a rate that grows
with rounds. Termination and truth are different properties; the round cap
defends the second, not the first.

### For review, the best-evidenced shape is a cold kill-round, not a conversation

The single most relevant paper to this design space is
[Refute-or-Promote](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.19049v1), a stage-gated
adversarial pipeline for defect discovery:

- Creative agents argue a finding is real; **cold-context adversarial agents
  receive only the claim — not the proposer's reasoning — and try to kill
  it**; nothing ships without an empirical check.
- Results: **79–83% of candidate findings were false positives** killed
  before disclosure. The cross-model critic stage caught bugs same-family
  review missed and found correctness issues in **~16% of
  same-family-approved fixes**.
- Its hardest-won lesson: **"unanimity is a low-signal event."** 80+ agents
  unanimously endorsed a nonexistent OpenSSL vulnerability (killed by one
  test), and a unanimous *kill* of a real bug later became a CVE. Agreement
  between LLMs is not evidence; only execution is.

Supporting results: [Cross-Context Review](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.12123)
found **fresh-session review beats same-session** (anchoring suppresses
criticism) and **cross-model beats same-model even at equal capability**.
MAD applied directly to code tasks shows
[mixed results](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.12029), and heterogeneous agent
*teams* often underperform their best member due to consensus-seeking over
expertise.

### Redaction is the cheapest large lever

A controlled bug-planting experiment
([Claude reviews codex's work, 96 reviews](https://medium.com/@ribrewguy/what-i-found-when-claude-reviewed-codexs-work-5d83a348a2d9))
found **framing is the biggest quality lever**: passing the implementer's
confident self-assessment to the reviewer **cut findings 3–4×** (9.4 →
2.4–4.0 per review) and halved critical-severity tags. Redaction discipline
mattered more than which vendor reviewed.

kolu independently discovered the same effect: `/lens-debate`'s own #1109
trial found that feeding hickey a *pre-curated* lowy finding to rebut
produced a *drop* verdict, while independent parallel review made hickey
raise the same issue on its own and flip the verdict to *fix* — "curation
biases the outcome; independent-then-debate does not"
(<Cite file=".apm/skills/lens-debate/SKILL.md" />). The literature and the
in-house experiment agree. The design consequence: redact the **author's
self-assessment and confidence language** from generation prompts, and
redact the **proposing reviewer's chain of reasoning** from verification
packets — but always include the full claim text and its cited code
locations, or responsible refutation becomes impossible.

### Structural-lens diversity is a bet, not a result

Cross-model diversity for *bug-finding* is real:
[CodeFuse-CR-Bench](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.14856) finds **no single LLM
dominates** across code-review dimensions; vendor benchmarks claim multi-model
review catches ~1/3 more issues with low overlap. But **no study directly
measures whether frontier models produce *different structural* findings**
(decomposition, coupling, volatility) — and a strong lens prompt like
hickey/lowy's may *homogenize* outputs across model families, shrinking the
diversity you're paying for while inflating false confidence when both
families "agree". The honest position: codex running the hickey lens is an
unproven bet, codex *verifying* hickey-lens claims is the evidenced role.
This is testable cheaply — run the kill-round retrospectively over ~10 past
merged PRs and count verified refutes/amends/duplicates before building
anything permanent on the generation side.

## The design tournament

Three designers worked from the same four evidence briefs under different
angles; nine adversarial critics attacked each design through one lens each
(cost/latency/maintenance, review quality, portability/failure-modes); two
judges scored the dossier with opposing priority orderings. Both judges
picked the same winner.

### Why debate-centric lost (3–3.5/10)

The most architecturally elegant reading of the debate literature — debate
only on disagreement, claims-only challenges, progress-gating — but its
central mechanism failed both judges' first criterion:

- Its **corroboration gate auto-applied precisely the findings most likely to
  be correlated false positives** (same diff, same lens catalog, two model
  families sharing the prompt) with *less* scrutiny than today, while
  "unanimity is a low-signal event" says agreement must never reduce scrutiny.
- Its **contested→No-op disposition reinstalled Defer through the back door**,
  with the incentive gradient pointing exactly opposite to the
  hard-won flip-to-Fix rule (three generations of prompt patching in agency:
  <Issue n={55} repo="srid/agency" /> → <Issue n={159} repo="srid/agency" />
  → <Issue n={160} repo="srid/agency" />): refute-then-stall costs two cheap
  calls; confirming obligates an apply+commit+verify chain.
- Its cost and quality premises were **mutually exclusive**: high cross-family
  finding overlap makes codex generation pure waste; low overlap routes most
  findings into per-finding debates with serialized warm sessions —
  re-deriving the <Issue n={1222} /> runaway with a termination proof
  attached.

### Why workflow-maximalist lost (4.5/10)

The most complete mechanization toolkit (schema'd no-Defer, role separation
in code, the only design confronting the re-review hole) — but as specced:

- ~19–22 agents and 3–5 codex execs per run, on the step that agency's
  timing data (<Issue n={128} repo="srid/agency" />) already shows is the
  slowest in 8/20 runs;
- a **round-1 gate that hard-failed the whole step on a single codex flake**,
  directly contradicting its own soft-degradation story;
- a stability loop that licenses lens ping-pong and manufactures convergence
  via gag-order prompts;
- a ~10-artifact maintenance surface across three languages with the findings
  schema duplicated — the exact mirror-edit failure mode it cited from kolu's
  own codex-debate history, shipped twice.

### Why ledger + kill-round won (7/10, both judges)

The winning design structures the *data* instead of rebuilding the
orchestration: a findings ledger every tier writes, one cold codex
kill-round replacing the weakest phase, and a thin straight-line workflow
wrapping exactly that sequence for Claude Code users.

- **The codex bet is bounded**: codex acts only on refute/amend/duplicate
  verdicts; confirms change nothing versus today; codex absence degrades to
  today's baseline verbatim. Worst case approximates the status quo.
- **Cost explosion is structurally impossible**: no loops anywhere; fixed
  agent count (5–6 vs. the maximalist's ~20); the zero-findings path is
  *cheaper* than today.
- **Three independently-landable increments** — a solo maintainer can ship
  the proven part before betting on the rest.
- Its critiques found genuine criticals — but every one converged on a
  point-fix (write-ahead ledger, symmetric evidence gate, resequencing)
  rather than an indictment of the architecture.

## The /do proposal

What lands in agency (and therefore in kolu's installed `/do` via APM), in
recommended shipping order. Full pipeline as in the diagram above; the parts
worth spelling out:

### The findings ledger is the spine

One gitignored `.do/findings.json`, written as a **write-ahead log**: stamped
with merge-base sha + run id, persisted at every phase boundary and after
*each* applied commit, with machine-readable provenance trailers (finding id,
lens, disposition) on every `refactor(hickey|lowy)` commit. Every consumer —
the PR-table renderer, the fallback path, `--from polish` re-entry — validates
the stamp and refuses stale state. This converts a crashed or interrupted run
from a wedge into a resume-by-content, and it makes the no-Defer rule
**structural**: the disposition field is a schema enum of
`fix-in-this-pr | no-op` — the rule that took three generations of prose
patching moves into a type that validation retries enforce.

### The codex kill-round

ONE cold `codex exec` per run (not per finding, not per round), reusing
kolu's `codex-exec-lib.sh` plumbing nearly verbatim (retry/backoff,
synthesized schema-valid error verdict, `--sandbox read-only`, the
MECHANICAL RUNNER agent pattern) while **dropping all multi-round
machinery** — no warm sessions, no resume, no rebuttal files. Effort `high`,
not `xhigh`: agency#97 shows `high` is the threshold where codex reviews find
real issues; <Issue n={1222} /> shows `xhigh`-every-round is the cost
runaway. The packet contains the diff plus each finding's full claim text and
citations, **redacting the implementer's self-assessment and the lens
reviewer's chain of reasoning** (the two evidenced correlated-error
channels). Codex returns one verdict per finding — `confirm`, `refute` (must
cite file:line), `amend` (the remedy as written creates a problem — this is
where the cross-lens question now lives), or `duplicate` (cross-lens
semantic dedup, for free).

### The evidence gate computes dispositions; nobody negotiates

- Verified refute (citation checked against the tree by a read-only
  verifier) → No-op with the evidence recorded.
- Unverified refute → the fix ships, table row marked **contested** with
  codex's objection verbatim — the human adjudicates at PR review, which is
  where `/do` already places the human. Contested findings are
  **apply-and-flag, never No-op**: a No-op-and-record disposition would be a
  schema-blessed Defer.
- The gate is **symmetric**: fix-bound findings need code-anchored citations
  too; confirm-without-evidence renders as such, never as verification.
- **Monotone degradation ladder**: codex absent *or failing mid-run* re-arms
  the legacy hickey⇄lowy cross-validation prose. A flaky codex can never
  produce less cross-checking than no codex. The PR comment header carries a
  mode badge (cross-family / single-family / codex-degraded) so review-depth
  variance is visible per PR.

### One bounded post-apply re-review

The biggest correctness hole in today's `/do`: the `refactor(*)` commits are
never reviewed by anyone. The fix is **one** pass scoped to the
refactor-commit diff, verdicts confirm/regress only, regressions fixed once
then frozen as flagged table rows — **no loop**. This closes the hole without
importing the <Issue n={1222} /> machinery. (kolu's `/be-review` has a
related structural answer for free — see below.)

### Cross-validation: keep, transformed, in two stages

The existing hickey⇄lowy cross-validation is the **only cross-check in the
whole option space with an empirically validated catch**
(<Issue n={198} repo="srid/agency" />). So: keep it on every tier now,
upgraded to run over ledger finding-ids (which mechanically kills its worst
defect — cross-validators re-emitting first-pass findings as new rows). Only
after a retrospective A/B shows the codex kill-round achieves a meaningful
verified kill/amend rate on *structural* claims does the cross-lens question
fold into the kill-round's `amend` verdict. The 79–83% kill rate in the
literature comes from refutable bug-claims; nobody has measured the transfer
to structural-taste claims. Don't trade the proven check for the bet before
the bet pays out. And when the question does move, it must move **with the
lens methodology loaded** — a generic verifier holding neither SKILL.md
cannot run the complecting catalog or the documented hickey/lowy
conflict-resolution pattern, which is the part that did the work in #198.

### Shipping order

1. **Ledger + deterministic table renderer + id'd cross-validation** — pure
   portability, no codex, no Workflow tool; fixes documented failures on
   every harness.
2. **The retrospective A/B** — a throwaway script over ~10 past merged `/do`
   PRs, *before* any codex infrastructure lands.
3. **`codex-crosscheck.sh`** (vendored kolu lib) — if and as the A/B
   justifies it; plain bash, benefits every tier.
4. **`review.workflow.js`** — last, gated on a smoke test and a non-silent
   tier-fallback signal. (~200 lines vs. `debate.workflow.js`'s 512 — no
   round loop, no warm sessions, no synthesis machinery.)

Cost at tier 1 with findings present: 5–6 agents, ~5–9 min — roughly today's
wall clock, with the two cross-validation forks replaced by one codex call
billed to the codex subscription, and reviewer prose no longer burning main
context.

## What kolu should change

kolu's review stack is ahead of the field in three ways the evidence
endorses: **structured JSON consensus detected in code** (not vibes),
**kernel-enforced read-only codex sandboxes**, and `/lens-debate`'s
**independent-then-debate** discipline (the #1109 lesson, independently
replicating the published anchoring results). The recommendations below keep
all of that and adjust the parts the evidence contradicts.

### /be-review: reorder the gauntlet, fix the rationale channel, add a cheap tier

The serial gauntlet (<Cite file=".apm/skills/be-review/SKILL.md" />) has a
property the research values highly and agency's `/do` lacks entirely: *"each
step sees the commits the previous step added… a later reviewer reviews the
earlier reviewer's fixes too."* That is a free re-review pass. But it is
currently pointed the wrong way:

1. **Run `lens` before `codex`** (today: codex → lens → simplify → police).
   `/lens-debate` applies *structural rewrites* — the largest, riskiest
   commits in the gauntlet — and in today's order nothing cross-model ever
   reviews them: only `/simplify` and `/code-police` follow, and neither is a
   correctness reviewer. Flipping to **lens → codex → simplify → police**
   makes the cross-family correctness debate cover the lens's applied
   refactors, closing the same unreviewed-refactor-commits hole that is the
   biggest defect in agency's `/do` — using machinery `/be-review` already
   has. The serial-editor collision argument is order-independent; only the
   coverage changes.
2. **Thread `--rationale` into adjudication, not generation.** Today the
   rationale is passed to the lenses "so the lenses don't flag deliberate
   decisions" — but author intent and confidence shared with reviewers is the
   measured 3–4× finding-suppression channel, and pre-loading reviewers is
   exactly the curation bias `/lens-debate`'s own #1109 trial proved changes
   verdicts. The deliberate-decision context belongs at the *disposition*
   step: let the lenses flag the deliberate decision cold, then let the
   rationale justify a `wont-fix` disposition during cross-examination —
   visibly, in the debate trail, instead of invisibly, as a finding that was
   never born.
3. **Add a `--quick` tier (and consider making it the default).** The full
   gauntlet runs two uncapped/high-backstop debates at xhigh/Opus per PR. The
   evidence-shaped cheap tier: independent lens generation (parallel, as
   `/lens-debate` already does) → merge with stable ids → **one cold codex
   verification of the merged findings** (claims-only packet,
   confirm/refute/amend/duplicate) → evidence gate → serial apply. Fixed
   agent count, one codex exec, no rounds. Reserve the full debating gauntlet
   for flagship changes where the marginal value of cross-examination is
   worth the marginal cost — and the marginal value is highest exactly when
   the cheap tier's verdicts come back *contested*, which the ledger makes
   visible.
4. **Make codex degradation honest and substituted.** Today, three
   `reviewer-error` attempts mean "give up on codex… move on" — the run
   silently loses its only cross-family check. Borrow the monotone-ladder
   rule: on persistent reviewer-error, substitute a fresh-context same-family
   correctness pass (fresh-session still beats same-session per
   Cross-Context Review), and badge the final comments with the degraded
   mode so review-depth variance is visible on the PR rather than absent.
5. **Share one findings ledger across tracks.** Four tracks post four
   comments and nothing dedups across them — codex and lens can flag the same
   construct, and police's elegance pass re-fixes what simplify settled.
   A run-scoped ledger with stable ids (the `.codex-debate/` scratch-dir
   pattern generalized), each track reading and extending it, would cut
   double-fixing, give `/be-review` crash-resume for free, and let the final
   comments render deterministically instead of being retyped per track.

### /codex-debate: cap the review loop, gate concessions on evidence

- **Adopt a round backstop in review mode.** `/lens-debate` already has the
  right shape — `--max-rounds` with `unresolved` as a first-class terminal
  state that `/be-review` knows how to adjudicate. Review-mode
  `/codex-debate` has neither ("no round cap, no deadlock exit"), and
  <Issue n={1222} /> documents the consequence. The literature adds the
  sharper point: even *converging* debates go increasingly wrong after round
  2. A backstop of 3 with `unresolved` reported honestly is not a deadlock
  exit — quitting without agreement *and pretending otherwise* is what
  defeats the purpose; surfacing genuine disagreement to the human is the
  correct output of a debate that didn't converge.
- **Evidence-gated concessions.** Add a required `concessionReason` (with a
  code citation) to both sides' schemas whenever a disposition flips across
  rounds. Smit et al. found agreement modulation is the highest-leverage MAD
  knob, and the conformity literature shows ungated flips are predominantly
  harmful. A side that cannot cite *why* it changed its mind hasn't been
  convinced; it has capitulated to end the loop.
- **Tier the reasoning effort.** xhigh on round 1, high thereafter — the
  <Issue n={1222} /> fix list already proposed this; agency#97 establishes
  `high` as the find-real-issues threshold.
- Answer mode (<PrLink pr={1269} />) shares the loop-to-agreement contract
  and should get the same two guards (backstop + concession reasons); its
  parallel-swap confirmation phase is sound and unaffected.

### /lens-debate: verify unanimity, tighten the backstop

- **Unanimity is the unguarded path.** Findings both lenses agree on
  round 1 lock as settled and get applied with *zero* adversarial check —
  yet hickey and lowy share training family, prompt style, and literally
  cross-reference each other's catalogs, so their agreement is the
  correlated, low-signal kind. Route agreed `fix` findings through the same
  single cold codex claims-only check before apply. Agreement should gate
  *debate* (skip it — the lenses have nothing to argue), never *verification*.
- **Debate only on disagreement** is already the implicit structure
  (settled-findings lock); make it explicit and pair it with a tighter
  backstop — `--max-rounds` default 3–4 rather than 12. The contested set is
  monotonically non-increasing, so almost all convergence happens in the
  first rounds; what's left at round 4 is a genuine judgment call that
  `unresolved` + human adjudication (which `/be-review` §2 already
  implements) handles better than rounds 5–12 of bias amplification.
- **Keep** independent parallel generation, the settled-findings lock,
  sequential reveal, and the electricity probe — all four survive contact
  with the literature, and the first is independently validated by it.

<Callout kind="warn" title="The one-sentence version">
Spend the debate budget where disagreement is (contested findings, capped
rounds, cited concessions), spend the verification budget where agreement is
(cold cross-family kill-rounds on claims-only packets), and never let either
budget be spent by a loop whose exit condition is two models getting tired
of arguing.
</Callout>
