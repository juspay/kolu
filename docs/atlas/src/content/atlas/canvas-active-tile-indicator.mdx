The canvas tile carries an **animated agent-status ring** on its border (marching
ants / sweeping comet, in the terminal's own theme colour — see
[canvas-tile-state-borders](/canvas-tile-state-borders.html)). That ring + the
repo-identity border colour mean the **border is spoken for**. The *active /
focused* tile must be marked on a **completely separate channel** — never another
coloured border.

<Callout kind="note" title="Two hard constraints (both learned the hard way)">
1. **Off the border.** Focus cannot be a ring, an edge accent, or anything that
   sits on the border the status comet travels — it reads as a second, competing
   border.
2. **Visible in every environment.** App mode (dark / light) and terminal theme
   (dark / light) flip *independently* → four combinations. The cue must survive
   all four — most punishingly a **light-themed tile (e.g. Gruvbox Material
   Light) on the dark canvas**, which is where the lift's black drop-shadow and
   then a faint white glow each disappeared.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="warning" title="The insight that should drive the choice">
An **absolute** cue — a mark that must be legible *on its own* (a shadow, a glow,
a strip in a fixed colour) — faces the four-combo contrast problem head-on and
has to be made *mode-aware* (read the app mode) or *theme-locked* (derived from
the tile) to survive. A **relative** cue — active simply *differs* from inactive
(the others dim, desaturate, or shrink) — sidesteps the problem entirely, because
within any single environment the two states only have to differ from each other,
which they always do. The relative options below (**D**, **G**, and the "recede"
half of **B**) are robust *by construction*.
</Callout>

Every tile wears its **agent-status comet** (the spinning border light). The
**middle** tile is the focused one — and it is deliberately a **light-themed
terminal** (Gruvbox-Material-Light-ish: cream bg, brown fg) so each option is
judged in the hard case. Both rows are the *same* option: **dark app mode** on
top, **light app mode** below.

## Today — and why it's out

<Pill variant="run">rejected</Pill> The focused tile got a teal accent on its
right edge + a 1px accent ring — sitting on the same border the status comet
travels, so it read as a **second border** fighting the first.

<p class="ai-cap">The teal edge + ring overlap the spinning comet — the exact clash that started this.</p>

## The options

### A — Accent title bar

Focus lives in the **chrome**: the title bar tints toward the accent + gains a
thin accent underline. Never touches the border. *Shipped first (<PrLink pr={1335}
/>) — then pulled:* the fixed accent tint **clashes** on themed tiles (it looked
wrong on a light tile and on Iceberg), and a tint over a light terminal bg barely
reads. An **absolute** cue with a theme-clash problem.

<AiPair opt="titlebar" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>A</b> · accent title bar — clean separation, but a fixed accent on a themed/light tile clashes or washes out.</p>

### B — Lift / elevation (mode-aware)

The focused tile **raises** and the others **recede** (dim). The lift is now
**mode-aware**: a dark drop-shadow on the light canvas, a bright glow on the dark
canvas (a black shadow is invisible on near-black). The *recede* half is relative
and always reads; the *lift* half is the absolute part that needed the mode-aware
fix. Calmest and most physical.

<AiPair opt="lift" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>B</b> · lift + recede — depth, no colour. The glow/shadow flips with app mode so it survives the dark canvas.</p>

### C — Outer accent glow

A tight accent **halo** hugging the tile edge. Different geometry from the
on-border comet, but it still puts the accent colour *right next to* the border,
so on a dense canvas it reads busy beside the comet — and the accent can clash
with the terminal theme. An absolute, accent-coloured cue.

<AiPair opt="glow" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>C</b> · outer accent glow — accent off the edge, but busy next to the comet and theme-clash prone.</p>

### D — Spotlight (dim the rest)

The inverse: **don't** mark the active tile — instead **dim + desaturate every
inactive tile**. The focused one is simply the only crisp, full-colour tile.
Nothing is ever added to the border, and because it's a **relative** cue it
survives every mode × theme with no tuning. Strongest on a busy canvas; subtle
when only one or two tiles exist.

<AiPair opt="dim" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>D</b> · spotlight — inactive tiles fade to grey; the active one is the only one in colour. Robust by construction.</p>

### E — Canvas spotlight pool

A soft **radial pool of light on the canvas** behind the active tile — like a
spotlight from above. It's drawn on the **canvas**, whose colour is fixed by app
mode alone, so it's mode-aware (bright pool in dark mode, a dark pool in light
mode) and **completely independent of the tile's theme**. The tile sits *in* the
light rather than wearing a mark.

<AiPair opt="pool" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>E</b> · canvas pool — the focus cue lives on the canvas, never on the tile, so the terminal theme can't break it.</p>

### F — Corner brackets (viewfinder)

Four accent **L-brackets** at the tile's outer corners — a targeting reticle, not
a continuous ring. Distinct geometry from the comet (corners only, and *outside*
the edge), and because the brackets sit on the **canvas** they only need to
contrast the (mode-known) canvas, not the theme. Crisp and unmistakable;
slightly more "UI chrome" in feel.

<AiPair opt="bracket" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>F</b> · corner brackets — a viewfinder reticle outside the corners; reads as "targeted", off the border, off the theme.</p>

### G — Scale hierarchy

The active tile **pops larger** (scale ~1.06); inactive tiles **shrink + dim**. A
relative, physical size hierarchy — the focused tile is literally the biggest.
Robust by construction (no colour, no absolute mark), but it nudges layout, so
it's best as a *small* scale paired with the recede.

<AiPair opt="scale" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>G</b> · scale hierarchy — the active tile is the largest; the rest step back. Pure relative size.</p>

### H — Accent top rail

A minimal cousin of A: just a **3px accent rail along the title bar's top edge**
(plus the accent dot), instead of tinting the whole bar. Lighter touch than A, so
it clashes less, but it's still an absolute accent mark and can fight a
warm-themed tile.

<AiPair opt="rail" />
<p class="ai-cap"><b>H</b> · accent top rail — a thin accent strip on the chrome; A's idea at lower volume.</p>

## How they sort

<Callout kind="note" title="Absolute vs relative — pick the axis first">
- **Relative (robust by construction):** **D** spotlight · **G** scale · the
  recede half of **B**. They only need active ≠ inactive, so no combo can break
  them. **D** is the cleanest of these on a busy canvas.
- **Off-tile absolute (mode-aware, theme-independent):** **E** canvas pool · **F**
  corner brackets. Drawn on the canvas, so they dodge the theme entirely and only
  need to track app mode — which we already know.
- **On-tile absolute (need care):** **A** title-bar tint · **C** accent glow ·
  **H** accent rail. All carry a fixed accent that can clash with the terminal
  theme; **A** and **H** also wash out on light tiles. **B**'s lift is on-tile but
  neutralised by going mode-aware.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="good" title="Where to look first">
If the cue should be **impossible to break across themes**, start with **D**
(spotlight the active by dimming the rest) or **E** (canvas pool) — both
sidestep the theme. If you want **depth without colour**, **B** (mode-aware lift)
or **G** (scale). The accent-on-chrome family (**A** / **H** / **C**) is the most
likely to clash and the reason the first attempt was pulled.
</Callout>
