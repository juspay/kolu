You leave a Claude Code session running for days because you're blocked —
waiting on a review, a deploy, a person. The agent is idle, but its terminal
still costs a PTY, an xterm buffer, a WebGL context, and a slot in your head. You
don't want to **close** it (that throws the thread away) and you don't want to
**keep** it (it's noise). **Sleep** is the missing third verb: put the terminal
to sleep now, **wake** it — in place, agent and all — whenever you're unblocked.

A sleeping terminal is *gone* the way a closed one is — no live process, no GPU
context, no xterm. What survives is a **record**: enough to respawn it in its
saved `$PWD`, replay its agent, and bring back its splits, theme, and canvas
position. That record is exactly what **session restore** already writes on
shutdown — so Sleep is not a new mechanism, it's **session restore scoped to one
terminal**, on demand.

This note is the plan of record for that feature. It pulls together four threads
from the brief: the core sleep/wake, a **sleeping tile that stays visible on the
canvas** (and in the Dock and switcher, in both view modes), the **symmetry with
a future "planned terminal"**, and a second-order payoff — once you can banish a
clamoring agent, every *awaiting* and *alert* signal is free to get **louder**.

## A sleeping tile is a dormant terminal

Strip away the verb and what Sleep produces is a **dormant tile** — a tile that
represents a terminal with no live process behind it. The whole feature is the
care and feeding of that one object: how it's born, where it shows, and how it
comes back to life.

That framing matters because a dormant tile is not unique to Sleep. A **planned
terminal** — intent you've jotted down but not yet spawned (<PrLink pr={760} />'s
*"where did I park that task?"*, <PrLink pr={859} />) — is the *same object* seen
from the other side of time. The two are mirror images:

<Svg svg={lifecycle} caption="One dormant-tile primitive, two producers. A planned tile has only a future (activate → start fresh); a sleeping tile has a past (wake → resume the agent). Sleep ships now; planned tiles are the deferred future sibling." />

<Callout kind="accent" title="Design for the shape, ship one producer">
Sleep and Plan differ in exactly two bits: a sleeping tile carries a
`lastAgentCommand` (so Wake *resumes*) where a planned tile carries only
`intent` (so Activate *starts fresh*); and a sleeping tile has scrollback history
a planned one never had. Everything else — the placeholder render, the canvas
slot, the Dock/switcher projection, the come-to-life seam — is shared. **We build
the dormant-tile rendering once, with Sleep as its only producer today, so the
planned-terminal proposal slots in later without a second silo.** We do *not*
build planned terminals here.
</Callout>

### Why Sleep / Wake, and nothing else

The metaphor everyone already holds for *freeze-now-resume-later* is a laptop:
you don't fold or stash it, you **sleep** it, and later you **wake** it. Three
things make it the right verb here, beyond familiarity:

- **The icon already agreed.** The dormant state reads best as a dimmed, moonlit
  tile — and ☾ *is* the sleep glyph. Verb and icon tell one story.
- **It sharpens a distinction we already owe the user.** kolu has `idle` and
  `parked` states for terminals that are *live but quiet*. The laptop model draws
  the exact line: an **idle** laptop is on with the screen dim; an **asleep** one
  has powered down to memory. Same word, same intuition, no new vocabulary to teach.
- **It avoids a collision.** "Park" is already taken by the Dock's `parked`
  bucket (`dockRowRanking.ts:26`); "Fold" reads as code-folding; "Suspend" is the
  jargon, not the word. Sleep is clean.

<Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill> — and the name is used end to end, with no
"suspend" or "fold" surviving in the internals: a `sleepingTerminals` cell,
`terminal.sleep` / `terminal.wake` RPCs, a `sleeping` Dock bucket. The spatial
quality the brief asked for survives as the *animation*: the tile collapses to a
compact sleeping card right where it sat.

## Reusing the session-restore spine

The persisted shape of a frozen terminal already exists and is load-bearing:
`SavedTerminal` (`packages/common/src/surface.ts:381`) is every persisted field
plus a stable `id`, and `SavedSession` is a list of them. Session restore writes
it on shutdown and replays it on boot. Sleep rides the same rails.

### Snapshot, persist, then kill — in that order

`snapshotSession()` (`packages/server/src/terminals.ts:62`) already converts a
live terminal to a `SavedTerminal` by stripping the three live fields
(`pr`, `agent`, `foreground`) off its metadata and keeping the rest. Factor a
**`snapshotTerminal(id)`** that does this for one terminal *and its sub-terminals*
(the splits, found by `parentId`). Then:

1. **Snapshot** the subtree to `SavedTerminal[]`.
2. **Persist** it (below) — *before* any teardown, so a crash mid-sleep never
   loses the record. This mirrors session.ts's "never kill-then-pray" discipline.
3. **Tear down** with the existing `handleKillWithSubs`
   (`useTerminalCrud.ts:201`), which kills splits then parent and frees the PTY,
   WebGL context, and xterm.

<Callout kind="good" title="Wake restores everything session restore restores — non-negotiable">
A woken terminal must be indistinguishable from one a full session restore
brought back: same `$PWD`, theme, canvas layout, splits, right-panel, intent, and
a resumed agent. Two split fields are client-only today (`activeSubTab`,
`focusTarget` in `useSubPanel.ts`) and would otherwise default to the first tab —
so we **persist them**, a one-place schema change that lifts *session restore's*
fidelity too (reuse the source of truth, don't fork it). The snapshot reads
`store.getMetadata` while the terminal is still live, because killing it disposes
the metadata owner.
</Callout>

### One new cell, two new RPCs

The record needs a home, and the repo has exactly one canonical mechanism for
persisted, streamed server state: a **surface cell** backed by the on-disk `conf`
store. The `session` cell is the template to clone, end-to-end:

| Layer | `session` (existing) | `sleepingTerminals` (new) |
|---|---|---|
| Schema | `SavedSessionSchema` | `z.array(SleepingTerminalSchema)` — `surface.ts` |
| Persist | `conf` key `session` | `conf` key `sleepingTerminals` + migration + `SCHEMA_VERSION` bump (`state.ts`) |
| Server writer | `session.ts` autosave loop | `sleepingTerminals.ts` — explicit, **no** debounce |
| Client read | `app.cells.session.use` | `app.cells.sleepingTerminals.use` (`wire.ts`) |

A sleeping record is one entry — the top terminal plus its splits, with a
`sleptAt` timestamp and a stable id (independent of any future live terminal id).
Crucially it lives in **its own cell, not `session`**: the moment Sleep kills the
live terminal, the next session autosave drops it from the `session` snapshot —
correct, but it means the two stores must be disjoint or a sleeping terminal would
be both restored-live and shown-asleep after a server restart.

<Callout kind="good" title="Sleep survives kolu restarts — and restores as sleeping">
The `sleepingTerminals` cell is on disk, so sleep is durable across restarts —
which is the whole point of sleeping something for days. On boot the two cells
hydrate side by side: `session` brings live terminals back **live**, and
`sleepingTerminals` brings sleeping tiles back **asleep**. A terminal that was
asleep at shutdown is *never* auto-woken — it reappears as a sleeping tile in its
slot, exactly as you left it, until you wake it yourself. The disjoint-stores rule
above is what keeps the two from fighting over the same terminal on restart.
</Callout>

The verbs are two request/response RPCs beside `terminal.kill`
(`contract.ts` / `router.ts`): **`terminal.sleep(id)`** and
**`terminal.wake(id)`**. Being plain request/response (not async iterators), they
need no `stream`-namespace wrapper — the cell's `get` already yields
snapshot-then-delta for free.

### Wake is restore-of-one

Wake barely needs new code. `handleRestoreSession`
(`useSessionRestore.ts:151`) already accepts an arbitrary blob through its
`options.session` seam (proven by `sessionTransfer.ts`), loops `handleCreate`
with the saved metadata, recreates splits, and **resumes the agent** via
`resumeAgentCommand(t.lastAgentCommand)`. Wake = hand it the one sleeping record,
then remove that record from the cell. Whether to call `handleRestoreSession`
directly or factor a shared `restoreOneTerminal` helper is an implementation
detail; the protocol is identical.

<Callout kind="danger" title="Local terminals only — the same fence restore lives behind">
Restore deliberately drops `t.location` and respawns every terminal at
`LOCAL_LOCATION` (`useSessionRestore.ts:248`). A remote (kaval) terminal would
silently wake *locally*. Sleep inherits this exactly: **do not ship Sleep for
remote terminals until the P3 dial-and-adopt path lands.** And if the saved
`$PWD` no longer exists on disk at wake, we **fail loudly** — surface the error,
never silently re-home (the codebase's fail-fast default).
</Callout>

## Where sleeping tiles live

A sleeping terminal is no longer in `store.terminalIds()`, so every Dock,
switcher, and canvas surface — all pure projections of the live list — would
simply *lose* it. The fix is one seam: merge the `sleepingTerminals` cell as a
second source at `useDockOrder` (the single memo all three surfaces read), and
teach the canvas to render sleeping records as placeholder tiles. The treatment
must read in **both** canvas mode (spatial) and maximized mode (no canvas — the
Dock is the home).

### On the canvas — it sleeps in its slot

`CanvasTile` is already a pure shell: geometry and chrome around a `renderBody()`
render prop, with a built-in `dimmed` state for tiles that should *recede*. A
sleeping tile is that shell at its saved canvas position, dimmed and moonlit, its
body swapped from an xterm for a dormant panel — intent, `$PWD`, the agent it will
resume, and a **Wake** button. And because there's no live grid to honor, it
**shrinks to fit that content** rather than holding the live tile's full
footprint: a small card marking the spot your work used to occupy, not a
full-size ghost. No PTY, no WebGL, just a static node.

<SleepingCanvas />

### In the Dock and the switcher

Add a `sleeping` value to `DockRowBucket` (`dockRowRanking.ts:26`) — the
`pipVariant` map is an exhaustive `Record`, so the compiler points at every site
that must handle it. A new ☾ pip and a `[data-bucket="sleeping"]` dimmed
treatment give it a distinct, at-rest look next to live rows. The switcher
(`WorkspaceGrid.tsx`) gains a **Sleeping** column. Both surfaces render in canvas
*and* maximized mode, so the sleeping terminal is reachable however you're
working.

### Putting one to sleep

Per the brief, three entry points — and notably *not* a buried one:

- **A per-tile button** beside the `×` in the title bar (`CanvasTile.tsx:352`) and
  on the Dock row — one click, always visible. The hero affordance.
- **A command + tip** — a "Sleep terminal" palette action registered once in
  `input/actions.ts` (palette-only; every `Mod+Shift` chord is already taken),
  plus a discoverability tip in `settings/tips.ts`.
- **An idle nudge** — when a terminal crosses the existing `48h+` idle bucket
  (`activityWindow.ts` / `staleness.ts`, reused, not reinvented), gently suggest
  sleeping it. <Pill variant="ok">yes</Pill>

## Sleeping licenses louder alerts

Here's the second-order payoff, and it's not small. kolu's *awaiting* and *alert*
signals are deliberately **restrained** today, because a user who can't escape an
alert will come to resent a loud one. Sleep removes that constraint. Once any
clamoring terminal is one click from gone, the signal is free to be **insistent**
— and *everywhere*, not just on one surface:

- the tile's border aura (`CanvasTile.tsx` `data-aura`, `index.css`) — a brighter,
  faster comet sweep for *awaiting you*;
- the Dock pip and its glow — bolder, with more contrast;
- the switcher's awaiting card;
- and the out-of-app surfaces the user asked to be louder too: the PWA badge, a
  title flash, an optional sound.

<AlertContrast />

In other words, Sleep isn't only a way to quiet the workspace — it's what *earns*
the right to make the un-quiet parts shout. The two changes are complementary and
should land together: ship the escape hatch, then turn up the alarm on every
surface at once.

## Decisions and scope

In scope: local terminals; one `sleepingTerminals` cell + `sleep`/`wake` RPCs; the
sleeping-tile render across canvas, Dock, and switcher; the three triggers; louder
awaiting/alerts on every surface. Out of scope: planned terminals (designed-for,
not built), remote-terminal sleep (gated on P3), and any task/kanban layer
(<PrLink pr={760} />).

The calls the brief settled — now locked:

- **Naming** — `Sleep / Wake`, used end to end; no "suspend"/"fold". <Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill>
- **Fidelity** — Wake restores *everything* session restore restores, plus the
  split's active-tab/focus (persisted, lifting restore too). Non-negotiable. <Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill>
- **Missing `$PWD` on wake** — fail loudly. <Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill>
- **Retention** — sleeping records persist indefinitely, exactly like terminals;
  no cap, no TTL. <Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill>
- **Idle nudge** — yes; suggest Sleep at the `48h+` idle bucket. <Pill variant="ok">decided</Pill>

- **Waking into an occupied slot** — every tile remembers its exact canvas
  position, and a sleeping tile keeps that saved spot; by the time you wake it,
  another live tile may have taken it. Wake returns the terminal to its saved
  position and nudges the occupant aside, reusing the existing placement engine
